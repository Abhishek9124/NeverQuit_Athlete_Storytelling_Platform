Para athlete Archery Paralympics 2024 6 min read
She has no arms. She holds the bow with her feet. She won gold.
SD
Sheetal Devi
Jammu & Kashmir Paralympics 2024 · Gold

Sheetal Devi draws a bow with her feet. This is not a metaphor, not a poetic image, not a figure of speech. She lies on her back on the shooting platform, grips the riser with one foot, hooks the string with the other, and releases — and the arrow flies with a precision that makes judges double-check the scoreboard.

She was born without arms. In a small village in the Kishtwar district of Jammu & Kashmir, where the mountains are high and the roads are difficult, she grew up the way millions of Indian children do — in a house where the electricity was not always on, where the world outside felt very far away, and where the question of what a girl with no arms would do with her life had no comfortable answer.

The government sports programme that discovered her in 2021 was not looking for archers. It was a general talent identification camp. Sheetal walked in — on her own, without assistance, carrying herself in that particular way she has, spine very straight, chin slightly up — and someone handed her a bow.

"People ask me how I aim without hands. I tell them: with my mind first. The feet are just the last step in a thought that started much earlier."

Her coach Kuldeep Vedalankar said later that he knew within ten minutes. "She had the stillness," he told reporters. "Most beginners are all movement. She was completely still. I have trained hundreds of archers. That stillness — it cannot be taught. It either exists or it does not."

It took three years to develop her technique. There was no manual. No coach had ever taught a person to draw a bow with their feet. They had to invent every drill, every correction, every method together. She essentially wrote a new chapter of the sport's history from scratch — in a remote district, without famous sponsorships, without the world watching.

At the 2024 Paris Paralympics, the world started watching.

The comeback timeline

Paris Paralympics gold — youngest Indian to win Paralympic gold, global headlines
What you can take from Sheetal's story
When there is no manual for what you're trying to do, you get to write it. That is power, not disadvantage.
Stillness under pressure is cultivated long before the moment it's needed. Build it in ordinary days.
The right programme, camp, or person finding you matters as much as your talent. Keep showing up in new rooms.
